Motivation

We want to understand the complete theory of a valued field \((K,v)\) endowed with an endomorphism \(\sigma \in \operatorname{End}(K,v)\). We will call the pair \((K,\sigma)\) a difference field, and say that it is inversive if \(\sigma\) is surjective. In this cases, \(\sigma\) induces “auxiliary” endomorphisms \(\sigma_\Gamma\) on the value group \(\Gamma_K\) and \(\bar{\sigma}\) on the residue field \(k\). In the classical philosophy of valued fields, then, the theory of \((K,v,\sigma)\) should be completely understood in terms of the theories of \((\Gamma_K,\sigma_\Gamma)\) and \((k,\bar{\sigma})\). More precisely, one aims to prove something like: given an elementary class \(\mathfrak{C}\) of valued fields with endomorphism, and \((K,v,\sigma), (L,v,\sigma) \in \mathfrak{C}\),

\[(K,v,\sigma) \equiv (L,v,\sigma) \iff (\Gamma_K,\sigma_\Gamma) \equiv (\Gamma_L,\sigma_\Gamma) \land (k,\bar{\sigma}) \equiv (l,\bar{\sigma}).\]

A typical example of such a class \(\mathfrak{C}\) would certainly include more restrictions on the behaviour of \(\sigma\) and \(v\); for example, for today I will only look at the isometric case, i.e. when \(v(\sigma(x)) = v(x)\) for all \(x \in K\) (in other words, \(\sigma_\Gamma\)) is trivial. When trying to prove a similar theorem, one naturally has to understand immediate extensions.

Given an extension \((K,v) \subseteq (L,v)\) of valued fields, we have natural embeddings \(k \subseteq l\) and \(\Gamma_K \subseteq \Gamma_L\). We say that the extension is immediate if these embeddings are surjective.

A proper immediate extension makes the above theorem tricky — in the pure valued field setting (without endomorphisms), think of the situation when you have an immediate algebraic extension, i.e. you add a root of some polynomial. Then the value group and residue field don’t change, but the theory of the valued field might, since you have solved a new equation. By Kaplansky theory, I will mean broadly “good understanding of immediate extensions”. Today, I want to explain what we know about Kaplansky theories of isometric valued difference fields, at least in the case of equicharacteristic \(0\). In particular, I want to focus on the case where \(\sigma\) is not surjective (which is similar to the algebraic situation of imperfect valued fields of equicharacteristic \(p\), in a sense).

Maximality

A good understanding of certain types of immediate extensions goes via proving an existence and uniqueness theorem about the maximal immediate such extension. For example, going back to 1942, we know by work of Kaplansky that if we look at a valued field \((K,v)\) of equicharacteristic \(0\), then there is a unique – up to isomorphism over \(K\) – valued field \((L,v)\) such that:

  • \((K,v) \subseteq (L,v)\) is immediate,
  • \((L,v)\) has no proper immediate extensions (i.e., \(L\) is maximal).

In particular, one has \(L \cong k(\!(\Gamma_K)\!)\).

We can refine this question a little bit by restricting the type of immediate extensions. We also know that there is a unique – up to isomorphism over \(K\) – valued field \((L’,v)\) such that:

  • \((K,v) \subseteq (L’,v)\) is immediate algebraic,
  • \((L',v)\) has no proper immediate algebraic extension (i.e., \(L’\) is algebraically maximal).

We can play the same game for a valued field with an endomorphism \((K,v,\sigma)\). Instead of looking at algebraic immediate extensions we will have to look at transformally algebraic immediate extensions, i.e. extensions generated by solutions of equations involving \(\sigma\). We will then call a valued field with endomorphism transformally algebraically maximal if it has no proper immediate transformally algebraic extension. Let’s focus on the isometric setting. The picture looks a bit more complicated, and it splits along two axes, namely whether \(\sigma\) is surjective or not, and whether \(\bar{\sigma}\) is surjective or not.

  \(\sigma\) surjective \(\sigma\) non-surjective
\(\bar{\sigma}\) surjective Case 1 Case 3
\(\bar{\sigma}\) non-surjective Cannot happen Case 2

Case 1. If both \(\sigma\) and \(\bar{\sigma}\) are surjective, we know from the first 2000’s that we have full control of immediate extensions.

Theorem. (Bélair, Macintyre, Scanlon) Suppose \((K,v,\sigma)\) is such that \((k,\bar{\sigma})\) is linearly difference closed. Then all its maximal immediate extensions are isomorphic over \(K\), and all its transformally algebraically maximal, transformally algebraic immediate extensions are isomorphic over \(K\).

(Indeed, we also understand the complete theories of these fields.)

Case 2. If both \(\sigma\) and \(\bar{\sigma}\) are not surjective, then the situation is radically different.

Proposition. Given a difference field \((k,\bar{\sigma})\) of characteristic \(0\) such that \(\bar{\sigma}\) is not surjective, and \(k = k_0\!\mid t\rangle\) is generated by a transformally transcendental element \(t\), there is an isometric valued field with endomorphism \((K,v,\sigma)\) with residue difference field \((k,\bar{\sigma})\) and such that \((K,v,\sigma)\) admits two non-isomorphic (transformally algebraically) maximal (transformally algebraic) immediate extensions.

The idea. If you know how to prove that a valued field of positive characteristic with imperfect residue field has two non-isomorphic algebraically maximal immediate algebraic extensions, the same idea works.

Case 3. If \(\bar{\sigma}\) is surjective, but \(\sigma\) is not, the situation is (as expected) halfway through; indeed, if \(\bar{\sigma}\) is linearly difference closed, being transformally algebraically maximal implies that \(\sigma\) is surjective. So we need to restrict the possible extensions, in a fashion already used by Chatzidakis and Hrushovski to find the model companion of fields with an endomorphism.

Definition. Let \((K,\sigma) \subseteq (L,\sigma)\) be an extension of difference fields. We will say that it is transformally separable if \(K\) is linearly disjoint from \(\sigma(L)\) over \(\sigma(K)\).

We will then say that an extension is transformally separably algebraic if it is both transformally separable and transformally algebraic. We will say that a valued field with endomorphism is transformally separably algebraically maximal if it has no immediate proper transformally separably algebraic extension. Then,

Proposition. Suppose \((K,v,\sigma)\) is such that \((k,\bar{\sigma})\) is linearly difference closed and \(\sigma(K) \subseteq K\) is relatively algebraically closed. Let \((K_i,v,\sigma)\), for \(i=1,2\), be such that:

  • \((K,v,\sigma) \subseteq (K_i,v,\sigma)\) is transformally separably algebraic and immediate,
  • \((K_i,v,\sigma)\) is transformally separably algebraically maximal,
  • \(\sigma(K_i) \subseteq K_i\) is relatively algebraically closed.

Then \(K_1 \cong_K K_2\).
